### For New Founders: You have the idea. You need it built.

You have validated the problem. You have talked to potential users. You know what the first
version of the product needs to do. What you do not have is a technical co-founder, a
development team, or a clear path from concept to working software. The typical outcome when
founders in this position hire freelancers is a codebase that works only for the person who
built it, an architecture that cannot grow, and a project that took twice as long and cost
twice as much as the original estimate.

Valnee works with non-technical founders from the first conversation. We convert your product
thinking into a technical plan, make every technology decision with a plain-language
explanation attached, build a launch-ready MVP that you fully own, and hand it over with
documentation that allows any competent developer to continue the work. We do not build for
the sake of building. Every feature in the first version has to earn its place.

## What We Actually Do

### MVP Development

MVP development at Valnee means building the smallest working version of your product that
can be used by real people and generate real feedback. It does not mean building a polished
product with every feature the market might eventually want. Before a line of code is written,
we define the scope in a written document that specifies what is included, what is explicitly
excluded, and what success looks like at the end of the engagement. The codebase lives in a
repository you control from day one. Most MVP engagements run between eight and twelve weeks
from scoping to a deployed, testable product.

### Product Architecture

Architecture work means making the foundational decisions about how your system is structured
before significant code is written — the technology stack, the data model, how services
communicate, how users authenticate, how the system will be deployed and monitored. These
decisions compound over time. A well-considered architecture makes every subsequent
development decision faster and cheaper. A poorly considered one does the opposite. Valnee
delivers architecture work as a documented decision record: each major decision is recorded
with the alternatives considered and the reasons for the choice. This document belongs to you
and informs every developer who works on the product after us.

### Codebase Rescue and Refactoring

When an existing codebase is slowing down a team — when adding features takes too long,
when deployments are fragile, when the original developer is the only person who understands
how things work — the answer is usually not a full rewrite. It is a structured, prioritized
set of targeted improvements applied in the right sequence. Valnee begins codebase work with
an audit: a systematic examination of the code, the architecture, the deployment process,
and the test coverage. The audit produces a written report with a prioritized list of
improvements. We then execute against that list in defined delivery cycles, with the client
reviewing working changes at the end of each cycle.

### Technical Co-Founder Support

Some early-stage companies need someone who can fill the technical co-founder role on a
fractional basis — not a developer who executes tasks, but a senior technical voice who
can evaluate build versus buy decisions, review vendor contracts, interview developer
candidates, define the initial architecture, and translate technical constraints into
language the team and investors can act on. Valnee provides this on an ongoing retainer basis
for founders in the six-to-eighteen month window before a full-time technical hire makes
sense. This is not a substitute for a long-term CTO. It is a bridge that helps companies
make good technical decisions and avoid expensive mistakes while that hire is being made.

### Team Augmentation

Team augmentation means adding one or two senior developers to an existing team for a defined
period. The developer works inside the client's workflow — attending standups, using the
client's tools, communicating through the client's channels — rather than operating as an
external contractor receiving task assignments. This model works for teams that have a strong
technical direction but lack bandwidth, or for teams that need a specific skill set for a
defined body of work. Valnee does not supply junior developers for augmentation. Every
developer placed in an augmentation engagement is senior level, with a documented background
and a defined onboarding process that minimizes ramp-up time.

### API and Integration Development

Most early products are not one system — they are a product connected to a payment processor,
a CRM, a data provider, an email platform, an analytics stack, and any number of third-party
services. The integration layer is where early-stage products most commonly fail under load
or under change. Valnee builds integrations that are documented, testable, and maintainable
by the client's team after delivery. Every integration engagement ends with a technical
specification document that covers the data flows, error handling, and the steps required
to add, modify, or remove a connected service. This is not a long-term maintenance contract
by default — the goal is to deliver work your team can own.

### Performance and Scalability

Performance work addresses specific, measurable problems: response times that have degraded
as user volume has grown, database queries that run at acceptable speed with a thousand rows
and unacceptable speed with a hundred thousand, infrastructure that was sized for a different
era of the product. Valnee approaches performance engagements empirically — we measure before
we change, and we measure again after. Every recommendation comes with data. Most performance
engagements run four to eight weeks and end with a written findings report and implemented
fixes, not an open-ended monitoring retainer.

### Product Delivery Management

Most technical agencies do not include real delivery management in their engagements. The
developer builds; the founder manages the agency. Valnee offers embedded delivery management
for projects that benefit from it — a delivery-focused practitioner who owns the project
timeline, manages scope decisions in writing, runs the review cycles with the client, and
ensures that what gets built matches what was agreed. This is available on engagements over
a defined minimum duration. For founders who have had the experience of managing an agency
instead of running their company, this service addresses that problem directly.

## How We Work

```
INTERNAL NOTE: Process overview section. Five steps, each with a 2-3 sentence explanation.
This section converts skeptical founders by making the invisible visible. Show where the client
is involved, where Valnee makes decisions, and what is produced at each step. Link to the
full process page for readers who want more detail.
```

Every Valnee engagement follows the same five-step structure, whether the project is a new
build, a codebase rescue, or a developer augmentation. The steps are adapted for the specific
situation but the underlying logic — scope clearly, assess honestly, build in cycles, document
continuously, review after delivery — stays the same.

**Step 1: Intake and Scoping**

The engagement begins with a structured 45-minute call. Valnee arrives with a pre-prepared
brief template. The client fills in what they know about the problem, the constraints, and
what success looks like. The output of this step is a written scope document the client
reviews and approves before any work begins. Scope changes after this point are handled in
writing, with a note on the time and cost implications before the change is accepted.

**Step 2: Assessment or Technology Selection**

For new builds, this step produces a technology decision record — a documented explanation
of the stack chosen and the alternatives considered. For existing codebases, this step is
a structured audit that maps what exists before recommending what to change. In both cases,
the output belongs to the client and is written in language that does not require a technical
background to understand.

**Step 3: Delivery in Defined Cycles**

Work proceeds in two-week delivery cycles. At the end of each cycle, the client reviews
working software — not a status report, not a slide deck, but a deployed increment they can
interact with. Feedback from each review shapes the next cycle. If a cycle produces something
that needs to be revised before moving forward, that revision is scheduled in the next cycle
with a documented explanation of what changed and why.

**Step 4: Documentation and Handoff Preparation**

Documentation is written as work proceeds, not compressed into the final week of the
engagement. The handoff package includes internal architecture documentation, API
documentation where applicable, a deployment runbook, and a recorded walkthrough of the
delivered system. The format of the documentation is agreed on during the scoping step. The
client does not receive a codebase and a zip file — they receive a system they can hand to
any competent developer and have it understood.

**Step 5: Post-Delivery Review**

Two weeks after delivery, Valnee schedules a structured review call. This is not a
sales call for the next engagement. It exists to resolve anything that is unclear,
underdocumented, or behaving unexpectedly after the client's team has had time to work
with the delivered system. The outcome of this call is a final sign-off document
confirming that the deliverables meet the agreed scope.

One of the most common mistakes in early-stage product development is building before
demand is established. Before you commit engineering resources to a first version of your
product, you need to know whether enough people care about it to warrant the investment.
A waitlist page is the most direct way to test that question. It collects email addresses
from people who say they want the product, gives you an initial user base to talk to, and
produces a concrete signal you can show to investors or use to time a build decision.

Valnee provides a free waitlist builder for founders who are in the pre-build stage. You
describe your product in plain language, and we generate a live, hosted waitlist page you
can share immediately. The page tracks visitors, collects emails, and gives you basic
analytics on interest over time. There is no code required, no design work required, and
no long-term commitment. It is a tool for founders who are deciding whether to build, not
for founders who have already built.

### How much does a project cost?

Project costs vary significantly by scope and engagement type. Most MVP builds run between
$15,000 and $45,000 depending on complexity, the number of integrations required, and the
state of the product definition at the start of the engagement. Codebase rescue and
refactoring engagements typically run between $8,000 and $25,000. Architecture and technical
advisory work is usually scoped as a fixed-fee engagement in the $3,000 to $8,000 range.
Valnee does not provide quotes without a scoping call because scope determines cost, and
scope cannot be determined from a brief description. If your situation requires a rough
estimate before a call, the ranges above are a reasonable guide.

### How long does a typical project take?

Timeline depends on what you are building. Most MVP engagements run eight to twelve weeks
from the completion of scoping to a deployed, testable product. Codebase rescue and
refactoring engagements run four to eight weeks depending on the size and state of the
existing codebase. Integration work typically completes in two to four weeks. Timelines are
defined in the scope document and Valnee is accountable to them. If circumstances arise that
affect the timeline, the client is informed immediately in writing with a revised projection
and an explanation of what changed.

### Do I need to be technical to work with Valnee?

No. Several of Valnee's clients have no technical background at all. For non-technical
founders, the communication format is adjusted accordingly — weekly written summaries instead
of pull request reviews, plain-language decision records instead of architecture diagrams,
and product reviews that focus on what the software does rather than how it is built. You
are not required to evaluate technical decisions; you are required to evaluate whether the
software does what you need it to do and whether the scope is proceeding as planned.

### What happens to the code after the project ends?

The codebase belongs to you. The repository lives in your version control account from day
one of the engagement, not in a Valnee account that gets transferred at the end. The
documentation and decision records belong to you. There is no vendor lock-in, no proprietary
tooling that requires Valnee's involvement to operate, and no license that restricts what
you can do with the delivered system. The explicit goal of every Valnee handoff is to produce
a codebase and a set of documents that allow any competent developer to continue the work
without a transition period from Valnee.

### Do you work with founders who have limited budgets?

Valnee is not the cheapest option in the market. Junior development resources at offshore
rates will always be less expensive on a per-hour basis. What Valnee offers is senior
practitioners, documented work, and accountability — and those things have a cost. If your
project budget is under $10,000, a standard Valnee engagement is probably not the right
fit. The exception is the developer-on-demand model, which provides hourly access to
vetted senior developers without the overhead of a full project engagement. If budget is
the primary constraint, [see the developer store](developer-store.md) for a more
flexible arrangement.

### What if I am not satisfied with the work?

Every delivery cycle ends with a client review. If something is not right, it is addressed
in the next cycle before work proceeds. The scope document defines what "done" looks like,
so there is an objective reference point for any dispute. If at the end of the engagement
the delivered work does not match the agreed scope, Valnee will either fix the gap or
refund the relevant portion of the payment. What Valnee will not do is accept scope changes
introduced after delivery and treat them as failures of the original scope. That distinction
is why the scope document and the review process exist.

### Can you maintain the software after delivery?

Valnee is not a managed services provider. Post-delivery support is limited to the two-week
review period that follows every engagement. For companies that need ongoing technical
support — bug fixes, minor feature work, monitoring — the options are either a retainer
arrangement through the developer-on-demand model or a quarterly advisory engagement through
the Technical Co-Founder Support offering. Both are described in detail on the
[services page](services.md). The default assumption at the end of every Valnee engagement
is that the client's team takes full ownership of the delivered system.

### How do I get started?

There are two paths depending on your situation. If you have a project you want to scope —
a new build, a codebase rescue, an architecture review — [join the waitlist](waitlist.md).
The waitlist intake form takes about five minutes to complete. Valnee reviews submissions
within two business days and responds with either a request for a scoping call or a note
that the project is not currently a fit. If you need a developer immediately for a specific
body of work, [see the developer store](developer-store.md), where availability is listed
and booking can happen within a few days of initial contact.
